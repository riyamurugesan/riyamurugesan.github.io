About Me


I'm from Matthews, North Carolina and am currently a student at UNC Chapel Hill, graduating in May 2028. I’m pursuing a double major in Biology and Computer Science to learn how to develop interdisciplinary solutions that utilize technology to address critical healthcare issues.

Last year, under the guidance of Dr. John Vavalle in UNC’s Heart Valve Clinic, I used Excel to determine if there was a relationship between prosthetic valve size and blood leak after transcatheter aortic valve replacement (TAVR) for 215 patients post-procedure. During my time in the clinic, I watched 20+ procedures, from TAVRs and catheterizations to ablations in the electrophysiology lab. After witnessing how integral computer models and imaging are for proper navigation during procedures – whether it be mapping electrical currents during ablation or maneuvering a catheter to the aortic valve, I was drawn to how computers can be used to optimize clinical outcomes.






My passion for medicine heightened after witnessing my close friend’s mother pass away from a heart attack – an event that gave a new meaning to the research I was conducting in UNC’s Heart Valve Clinic. Despite witnessing multiple procedures that reduce the risk for myocardial infarctions, I saw firsthand that these interventions sometimes arrive too late, after a patient's underlying risk factors have caused serious harm. This realization, combined with my interest in biology, inspired me to analyze the body’s internal mechanisms to better understand and mitigate patient risk.

Currently, I am a member in both the Bautch Lab and Cardiovascular Modeling and Simulation laboratory, where I support research that uses biological and computational approaches to analyze blood flow and valve anatomy and their effect on cell behavior and valvular pressures, respectively. In the future, I hope to develop models to refine pre-procedural planning and reduce patient’s risk for thrombosis.
